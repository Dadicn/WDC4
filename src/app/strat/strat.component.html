Four strategies for simplicity


Remove
Organize
Hide
Displace

Each of the strategies has its strengths and weaknesses, which I’ll discuss in the following lines. A big part of success comes in
choosing the right strategy for the problem at hand.

Remove

The most obvious way to simplify is...
to remove what’s unnecessary

Conventional wisdom says that more features mean more capability which, in turn, means a more useful product. But these examples
choose depth of capability rather than breadth. They’re useful because they do a few things far better than their rivals.

Conventional wisdom also says that products with more features will beat products with fewer. But all of these examples have
competed against more fully featured rivals and won.

Removing clutter allowed designers to focus on solving a few important problems really well. It also allowed users to focus on meeting
their goals without distraction.

How to remove?

Focus on what’s core
- Removing clutter helps users focus on what’s important

Kill lame features
-We tenk to keep things, even when we know they’re broken

Prioritizing features
- Prioritize feature that satisfy mainstreams’ needs with minimal effort

Organize

Organizing is a great strategy for simplifying.

There are plenty of options open to you in organizing an interface— size, color, position, shape, hierarchy. But those choices need to be
employed with restraint.

If you want to organize for simplicity, it’s important to emphasize just one or two important things. Simple organization doesn’t draw
attention to itself, it lets users focus on what they’re doing.

How to organize?

Chunking
- Chunking is used throughout user interface design. The classic advice here is to break items down into groups of “seven
plus or minus two.”

Hard edges
-When you need to organize a group of items that are equivalent (like books in an online store), choose clear categories with labels
that make sense to your audience.
Grids
-It’s remarkable how far a tidy layout can go in making a design feel simple. The form on the opposite page (top) is an interface for searching for
train tickets that my company designed. It worked fine in user testing, but people hesitated over it. We revisited the
layout and decided we could simplify it.

Hide

Make sure your users will encounter a clue on their journey

There are plenty of options open to you in organizing an interface— size, color, position, shape, hierarchy. But those choices need to be
employed with restraint.

If you want to organize for simplicity, it’s important to emphasize just one or two important things. Simple organization doesn’t draw

How to hide?

Hide one-time settings and options.

Hide precision controls, but let expert users choose to keep them revealed.

Don’t force or expect mainstreamers to customize, but offer this option for experts.

Hide elegantly; that is, hide completely and reveal just in time.

Displace

The fourth strategy is to cheat?!

Designers who take this approach strip the site down to a few basic actions.

Also, though you’ve simplified the site, you still have the problem of designing a simple on-screen menu system (using the strategies
of remove, organize, and hide).

But if you understand the trade-offs, displacing the right roles to the right devices works well. One of the secrets of creating simple
experiences is putting the right functionality on the right platform or part of the system.

How to displace?

You can reduce complexity in software by designing features that can be used for several purposes. The task of choosing how to use
them is displaced to users.

The ultimate in simple interfaces are ones that make sense to experts and mainstreamers alike.

You need a sense of certainty to be able to displace tasks effectively.

If you’re going to displace tasks to be the responsibility of the user, you have to trust that the user will take on those tasks.

Simplicity happens in the user’s head!
